With interest rates shifting and housing inventory at near-historic lows, 2025 is shaping up to be one of the most complex years for real estate buyers and sellers in recent memory. Here's what the data tells us — and what it means for your next move.

Why Inventory Is Still the Biggest Problem

The fundamental issue driving everything else in the 2025 housing market is supply. The United States is facing a structural shortage of homes that has been building for over a decade. New construction starts have consistently fallen short of demand since the 2008 financial crisis, and that gap has never been properly closed.

In major metropolitan areas like New York, Los Angeles, and San Francisco, zoning restrictions continue to limit the construction of new multi-family housing. Meanwhile, existing homeowners who locked in historically low mortgage rates between 2020 and 2022 are reluctant to sell — a phenomenon economists are calling the "lock-in effect." These homeowners face a significant rate penalty if they move, which keeps even more inventory off the market.

"We're not just dealing with a short-term supply problem — we're dealing with the compounding effects of fifteen years of underbuilding in a country that added 30 million new households over the same period." — National Association of Realtors, Q1 2025 Report

Where Prices Are Heading

Despite the affordability challenges, prices in most markets are not falling — and in many high-demand areas, they continue to climb. The Sun Belt markets remain particularly competitive, with cities like Austin, Dallas, Nashville, and Miami seeing continued inbound migration and job creation that sustains demand even at elevated price points.

What Buyers Should Do Right Now

If you are actively looking to buy in 2025, the first and most important thing you can do is get pre-approved for a mortgage before you start touring properties. In today's market, sellers receive multiple offers on desirable properties within days of listing. A buyer without financing in place is immediately at a disadvantage.

Three Practical Steps for Today's Buyers

  • Lock in your rate as soon as you have an accepted offer — rates can move by 0.25% in a single week
  • Expand your geographic search radius by even 10–15 miles — this can reduce purchase prices by 20–30% in many metro areas
  • Consider new construction — builders are currently offering rate buy-downs and incentives that are not available in the resale market
  • Work with an agent who has access to off-market or pre-market listings — a significant percentage of homes sell before they ever hit Zillow

The Rental Market as an Alternative

For buyers who find ownership out of reach at current prices and rates, the rental market in 2025 offers some relief. Rent growth has slowed significantly compared to the 2021–2022 spike, and new apartment supply in many cities has put modest downward pressure on rents. However, this is very market-specific — cities with tight zoning continue to see elevated rents.

Our Outlook for the Rest of 2025

Dream Home's analysis team expects home prices to rise by an additional 4–6% nationally through the end of 2025, with rates potentially softening to the 5.8–6.2% range if the Federal Reserve proceeds with expected cuts in the second half of the year. For buyers who can act now, waiting may mean competing at higher prices with the same rate environment.

The bottom line is this: if you find a home that fits your budget and your life, 2025 is a reasonable time to buy. Time the market perfectly and you may save a few percentage points — but you also risk pricing yourself out entirely as inventory stays tight.
